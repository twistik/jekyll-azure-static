KTM 790 duke review

Twitter

  • KTM 790 duke review

The Orange team set out to build a mid-sized naked with decent power and precise handling - a mountain destroyer if you will.I'd say KTM succeeded.

As is usually the case with performance-orientated KTM, the overall design goal was to make the 790 Duke compact and lightweight. This started with the all-new 799cc parallel twin. It gets a lightweight, one-piece forged crankshaft with a 75-degree pivot, giving the 790 Duke a V-twin sound and a personality familiar to the Duke family. Forged pistons and connecting rods help reduce vibration and weight, smaller cam lobes and a secondary balancer shaft driven by the exhaust cam. Output is 95.1 hp at 9,200 rpm and 58.2 lb-ft of torque at 7,900 rpm on the dyno; by comparison, the Yamaha MT-09 produced 105.4 hp at 9,980 and 60.5 lb-ft of torque at 8,460 rpm. What the numbers don't convey is how freely the 790 spins.

The 790 Duke's throttle opening suggests that actual performance isn't much different between the two, although with the parallel twin available power from 3500 rpm. Initial power delivery at low revs is smooth, even if the transition between open/closed throttle is sometimes too sensitive in sporty low-speed riding mode - a quick switch to street mode solves the problem. Power comes on strong in the mid-rpm range when the motorcyclescle accelerates and only tapers off around its 9500rpm red zone. The Quickshifter + KTM adds to the excitement by allowing wide throttle opening with the Duke six-speed transmission at a good interval. What does the + mean? It also automobiles automatically for downshifting without a clutch.

KTM has carried over its responsive Traction Control (MTC) and ABS assistant, which work in conjunction with Bosch's five-axis IMU system to provide precision measurement that helps the Duke 790 from the larger 1290 LC8.Traction control settings are set in three of the available riding modes, while Race mode offers nine levels of adjustability. In addition, a feature called Engine Slip Regulation (MSR) works as a traction control system that prevents rear wheel braking when motorcycles are braking by opening the throttle as required. Seriously gimmicky, especially for the average neuked.

What does this mean for Duke? Think of a set of electronics as the ultimate riding coach, speeding you up instantly. No time wasted. The traction control system does a good job only as needed to soften the wheel on medium settings, but isn't aggressive enough to kill acceleration when coming out of a corner. While the highest TC settings hinder the 790's acceleration, the strong intervention suggests that the Duke will have no problem winning when the tarmac is soaked with rain. MSR is making a big move in keeping KTM in shape, too. The shift change to the fast sequence comes without too much thought, with the system working smoothly to keep the wheels in line. Go forward, go deep into that corner.

The Duke's chassis provides a confident feel throughout the turn. With a full load of 414 pounds, the 790 handles difficult side-to-side transitions with ease. The steeper the road, the more the Duke thrives. KTM's choice of Maxxis Supermaxx ST tyres help here, providing grip and feel from corner entry to exit.

The suspension components are obvious kryptonite for the 790, but only because of their non-adjustability, with the exception of the stepped rear spring adjuster. That's not to say that the 43mm WP fork and damper don't offer well-chosen settings that provide good balance or that there isn't a pretty solid overall feel, but front and rear the suspension is stiff and incompatible for roads with bumps . The Duke has a tendency to bounce under you and ride comfort suffers when the tarmac is less than ideal. Frankly, the Duke's lack of suspension adjustment is disappointing.

The feel of the KTM's brake calipers is also lacklustre. Initial lever tension gives a strong response, but nearing the apex, the lack of feel makes it difficult to know how much braking force is being applied. Good thing the Duke is equipped with ABS, which intervenes almost imperceptibly when you touch the brake.

Comfortable ergonomics make up for the 790's suspension setup errors, even if the overly stiff saddle irritates your buttocks after more than half an hour of riding. We found that the narrow chassis makes the 32.5-inch seat height lower, so the Duke is easy to handle in stop-and-go traffic or when parking. Still, there's plenty of legroom; the long reach of the relatively low handlebar gives the Duke 790 an aggressive feel, but it's not so extreme that it puts uncomfortable pressure on your wrists.
