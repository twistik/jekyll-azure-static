New Kawasaki 2019 zx6r

Ninja drives like a Cadillac, blissfully floating around with a fair amount of balance and control

Just like smartphones, the price of mid-weight sportbikes has steadily increased in recent years. Finally, Kawasaki says enough is enough , slashing the price of its Ninja ZX-6R supersport ($9,999 without ABS), giving it a facelift and adding a few minor upgrades for riders.

Kawasaki is clearly not reinventing the wheel, sticking with the more street-orientated and still plus-sized 636cc formula it introduced six years ago for the 2013 model year. Unlike some of its rivals, the ZX-6R is a civilised sports motorcycles that is equally good on the track and on the street.

It's powered by a 636cc Green Team designed engine that offers a wider and more powerful torque band than a typical supersport. Yes, the engine is still best at high revs, but it is less sensitive to gearing. The engine doesn't have to scream to accelerate confidently , adding some flexibility when you do manoeuvres.

Last time the ZX-6R (2013 MY) was tested, it only produced a figure of 112 hp at 13,500 rpm and 46.37 lb-ft of torque at 11,375 rpm.

The electronic quickshifter adds to the fun by allowing full-throttle gear changes through the six-speed transmission. A six-tooth (15-tooth) intermediate shaft gear reduces the main gear ratio and further increases acceleration. However, in top gear at 65 mph, the engine doesn't rev excessively, registering around 6,000 rpm on the tachometer hand. The shorter gearing certainly helps acceleration, especially in the first three gears, but the electronic shifter speed could be quicker, as there's a noticeable pause between shifts. It's certainly better than a manual setting, but most of the aftermarket tests we've tested have worked better lately

The addition of an automatic downshift function is welcome. To be fair, the transmission runs through each of the six gears smoothly, with responsive and light clutch action. The clutch lever position is now also adjustable, like the brake lever, although as it is, non-adjustability was never an issue.

The instrumentation is updated to include a fuel meter and economy features in the LCD indicator, allowing you to keep track of your 4.5 gallon fuel tank capacity. The meter needle also includes a great shift light. It can be set in 250 RPM increments so you know when it's time to shift into another gear

Kawasaki's three-way traction control (KTRC) is as portable as the engine's dual mode power modes. We rode the motorcyclescle in ‘full’ mode, but we'd recommend the low-power setting for a new rider or perhaps a new owner who wants to control their new bike more efficiently.

Aesthetically the ZX-6r uses a new top view and tail section that resembles its smaller brother the Ninja 400. Added are two LED headlights and other subtle touches like a new muffler trim, however it's bad for the motorcycles itself to look like one half its price (Ninja 400). So while we appreciate its family line, it would have been nice if the designers had done a customised and more aggressive look.

Weighing in at 428lbs (without ABS), the 6R is a lightweight motorcyclescycle with nice weight distribution and balance. It's a nimble bike that goes exactly where you tell it to go.

The suspension is softer than most other sports motorcycles, but its ride quality, especially on the road, is one of its best features. Both the fork and rear amort offer damping, but at the track, the setup didn't offer much gain in terms of lap times.

Ninja drives like a Cadillac, blissfully floating around with a fair amount of balance and control. Adding more damping made for a more controlled ride.

While the flamboyant 600cc class supercasters aren't as popular as they once were, it's important to remember that the Green Team has been diligently building these mechanical masterpieces for 35 years. Tradition is key and Kawasaki remains committed to the segment, walking a fine balance between performance, style and cost.
