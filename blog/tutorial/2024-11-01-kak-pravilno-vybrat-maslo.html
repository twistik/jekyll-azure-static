How to choose the right oil for your automobiles or motorcycles

Choosing the right motorcycles oil for your automobilesmobile can seem daunting, but the best way to start is to check your manual for your automobilesmobile manufacturers. Adjust that weight for the weather (more on that later), and then proceed to choose a specific brand of motorcycles oil by checking the star symbol, which indicates that the oil has been tested and meets American Petroleum Institute (API) standards. The container also has a 2 character service designation. The latest API service standard is ‘SL.’ SL refers to a group of laboratory and engine testing, including the latest series for high-temperature deposit control. Your third task is to select a viscosity (thickness) suitable for the temperatures your automobilesmobile normally operates in (check your manual), and you're all set. Well, not quite. It's much more than a story.

Understand the Labels

These are the labels you'll find on every container of reputable motorcycles oil. The API donut on the right tells you whether the oil meets the current SL (C for diesel engines) service level. It also gives the SAE (Society of Automotive Engineers) viscosity name and tells you if the oil has passed an energy conservation test. The asterisk symbol on the left indicates that the oil has passed the tests listed for SL service.

Is oil really the lifeblood of an engine? This has long been a popular analogy, but it's really not an accurate description. Blood carries nutrients to the cells, but it's air that carries the fuel - the ‘power’ - for the engine. However, without oil to lubricate and cool the moving parts, keep them clean and help seal the pistons in the cylinders, the engine will run for a few seconds and then sit. So, yes, oil is important.

Oil is so important that we want nothing less than the best engine we can get—at a good low price, of course. Now, what if you could have oil custom-made so that it has exactly the characteristics you believe are necessary for your vehicle and your type of driving?

To find out which viscosity to choose for your automobile, check the owner's manual for the oil viscosity chart.

Oil classification codes: The starburst symbol on the oil container label indicates that the oil meets the current engine protection standard and fuel economy requirements of the International Lubricant Standardization and Approval Committee (ILSAC), a joint effort of American and Japanese automobile manufacturers.

Synthetic oil: Some claim that synthetic oils allow for longer intervals between oil changes, result in less engine part wear, and perform better at higher engine temperatures. However, the claim of extended intervals has yet to be proven.

Viscosity

Viscosity (the resistance of a fluid to flow) is measured at 0°F (denoted by the number preceding the "W" [for winter]) and at 212°F (denoted by the second number in the viscosity rating). Thus, 10W-30 oil has lower viscosity when cold and hot compared to 20W-50. Motor oil thins out when it heats up and thickens when it cools. With the right additives to help it resist thinning too much, oil can be rated for one viscosity when cold and another when hot. The more resistant it is to thinning, the higher the second number (e.g., 10W-40 vs. 10W-30), and this is good. Thicker oil generally seals better and maintains better lubrication between moving parts.

On the low-temperature end, the oil must resist thickening so that it flows more easily to all the moving parts of your engine. Additionally, if the oil is too thick, the engine requires more energy to turn the crankshaft, which is partially submerged in the oil bath. Excessive thickness can make it harder to start the engine, reducing fuel efficiency. A 5W oil is typically recommended for winter use. However, synthetic oils can be formulated to flow even more easily in cold conditions, allowing them to meet the requirements for a 0W rating.

When the engine is running, the oil heats up. For example, the second number in the viscosity rating—"40" in 10W-40—indicates that the oil will remain thicker at high temperatures compared to one with a lower second number—"30" in 10W-30, for instance. What truly matters is that you use the oil viscosity recommended by your vehicle's owner's manual.
